MEMO CONTENT CHECKLIST
======================
Executive Strategy Memo — Compensation Tax Planning Tool
(Track 1 | 5 pages max, double-spaced)

--- SECTION 1: BUSINESS PROBLEM / ECONOMIC DECISION ---
- State the problem: early-stage companies and executive hires face a complex array of comp structures (cash, equity, options) whose after-tax value varies dramatically depending on the form, timing, and tax situation of both employee and employer
- Identify the audience: MBA students, startup founders, CFOs, or candidates negotiating an executive offer who need to compare comp packages on an apples-to-apples after-tax, NPV basis
- Explain why raw pre-tax numbers mislead: a $1M NQO grant, a $1M ISO grant, and $1M in restricted stock have very different after-tax values for both employee and employer

--- SECTION 2: TAX MECHANISMS AT PLAY (All Taxes) ---
- Cash salary/bonus: taxed as ordinary income (federal + state + FICA/Social Security wage base cap + 0.9% Additional Medicare above $200k threshold)
- Restricted Stock (RS) — no 83(b): taxed at ordinary rates on FMV at each vest tranche; employer gets deduction at vest
- Restricted Stock (RS) — with 83(b) election: tax paid at grant on then-current FMV; future appreciation taxed at LTCG rates (20% federal + state); employer deduction taken at grant; capital loss treatment if stock declines below grant FMV
- RSUs: ordinary income at vest (time-based or double-trigger liquidity event); no 83(b) available; FICA applies
- ISOs (§422): $100k annual limit (§422(d)) splits excess shares into NQO treatment; modeled as disqualifying dispositions (sell-on-exercise) → ordinary income, no AMT preference; employer gets deduction on disqualifying disposition spread
- NQOs: spread at exercise is ordinary income + FICA for employee; employer deducts spread × (1 + employer FICA) with corporate tax shield
- AMT: tracked at 26% rate; relevant for qualifying ISO exercises (hold-and-sell scenario)
- NIIT (3.8%): applies to net investment income; toggled per scenario
- Corporate tax rate (21%): reduces employer's net cost on deductible comp; §162(m) $1M deduction cap for covered employees is modeled as a toggle
- State taxes: separate ordinary and LTCG rates layered on top of federal; tool defaults to California rates (9.3%) as illustration
- LTCG vs. ordinary income differential: core tax planning lever, especially for RS 83(b) and ISO qualifying dispositions

--- SECTION 3: ALL PARTIES FRAMEWORK ---
- Employee perspective: after-tax cash flows discounted at employee's personal discount rate → Employee NPV
- Employer perspective: gross comp cost minus corporate tax deduction on eligible amounts, plus employer FICA, discounted at corporate hurdle rate → Employer NPV Cost
- The tool surfaces both simultaneously — a structure that minimizes employee taxes may also reduce the employer's deduction (e.g., qualifying ISO hold), illustrating the negotiated split of the tax pie
- §162(m) scenario: if the deduction is lost on cash comp above $1M for covered employees, the employer's after-tax cost rises materially — the tool models this toggle

--- SECTION 4: THE STRATEGIC SOLUTION (Tool Features) ---
- Web application built with React/TypeScript; runs locally; persistent storage via SQLite
- Users configure one or more named "packages," each with: company valuation, shares outstanding, planning horizon (years), stock price growth rates (downside / base / upside scenarios), salary growth rates, and bonus growth rates
- Comp components available: Cash Salary, Cash Bonus (custom payment schedule), Restricted Stock (with/without 83(b)), RSUs (time-based or double-trigger), ISOs, NQOs
- Custom vesting and exercise schedules: users can define precise month-level vesting or exercise events rather than defaulting to standard 4-year cliff/ratable
- Tax inputs panel: fully configurable federal, state, FICA, AMT, NIIT, corporate, discount rates — can be set globally or overridden per package
- Year-by-year table: shows stock price, employee after-tax cash, employer net cost, and per-component breakdown for each scenario
- NPV summary: Employee NPV and Employer NPV Cost for each scenario (downside / base / upside); marginal NPV column isolates each component's contribution
- Calculation transparency: NPV formula section shows the exact present-value math applied
- Package comparison view: side-by-side NPV comparison across multiple configured packages for the same scenarios
- Vesting timeline visualization: graphical representation of when equity events (vest/exercise) occur across the horizon

--- SECTION 5: ALL COSTS / RISKS & LIMITATIONS ---
- Non-tax frictions to discuss:
  - 83(b) election requires filing within 30 days of grant; no extension; failure forfeits the election permanently
  - ISO hold requirement (>1 yr from exercise, >2 yr from grant) creates illiquidity risk; model currently assumes disqualifying disposition for simplicity
  - Liquidity risk for pre-IPO equity: RSU double-trigger vesting and RS may vest with no market to sell — cash tax liability with no liquid proceeds
  - AMT exposure on qualifying ISO exercises: model currently uses disqualifying disposition; AMT risk on hold-and-exercise scenario is noted but not fully quantified
  - Exercise price financing: exercising options requires cash outlay; model does not model borrowing costs or cashless exercise mechanics
  - State sourcing rules: multi-state residents face allocation issues (e.g., equity granted in CA but exercised after relocating); tool uses a single blended state rate — a simplification
  - §409A valuation risk for RS/option grant prices at private companies
  - §162(m) grandfathering and transition rules are nuanced; the toggle is a binary on/off approximation
  - Clawback, vesting acceleration, and change-of-control provisions are not modeled
  - Tool assumes sell-on-vest/exercise for all equity — does not model hold decisions or basis tracking for subsequent sale
  - Discount rate selection is subjective; results are sensitive to this assumption (show sensitivity)

--- SECTION 7: KEY TAKEAWAY FRAMING (for memo conclusion) ---
- The tool makes the tax trade-offs legible: a high-equity package with 83(b) + ISO structure maximizes employee after-tax NPV in the upside scenario but creates illiquidity and AMT risk; a cash-heavy or NQO structure is more predictable but gives up LTCG treatment
- Employer perspective matters: NQOs generate a corporate deduction on exercise spread (benefiting the employer); ISOs with qualifying disposition do not — this difference is material in high-growth scenarios and should inform negotiation
- The "right" structure depends on growth expectations, employee liquidity needs, employer tax position, and state of residence — the tool lets both parties explore the parameter space interactively
