SECTION 1 — CASH SALARY & BONUS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1.1 Ordinary Income Tax on Wages
  Calculation: salary × (1 − federalOrdinaryRate − stateOrdinaryRate − ficaEffective)
  Authority:
    - IRC §61(a)(1): gross income includes compensation for services
    - IRC §1(j)(2)(D): 37% top marginal rate enacted by TCJA (P.L. 115-97); the TCJA
      individual rate provisions were subsequently extended by Congress and remain in
      effect for tax year 2026. The 37% default is verified as correct.
    - Treas. Reg. §1.61-2: wages, salaries, commissions as compensation income

1.2 Employer Deduction for Salary/Bonus
  Calculation: salary × (1 + ficaEmployer) × (1 − corporateRate)
  Authority:
    - IRC §162(a): ordinary and necessary business expense deduction for reasonable compensation
    - Treas. Reg. §1.162-7: reasonableness of compensation

1.3 IRC §162(m) Deduction Cap — FIXED
  Calculation: deductible portion capped at $1,000,000 cumulative per year; excess is
  non-deductible. Applied across ALL compensation components in component processing
  order via the YearContext.deductibleCompAccrued accumulator (engine/index.ts).
  Authority:
    - IRC §162(m)(1): disallows deduction for compensation >$1M paid to covered employees
      of publicly held corporations
    - TCJA §13601 (P.L. 115-97): eliminated the performance-based compensation exception
      effective for tax years beginning after 12/31/2017; ALL forms of compensation
      (cash, NQO spread, RSU vest income, RS vest income) paid to covered employees at
      publicly held corporations are now subject to the $1M annual cap
    - IRS Notice 2018-68: transition guidance on post-TCJA §162(m) application
  Implementation: deductibleCompFor() in tax.ts applies the cap across all comp types.
  Employer FICA (IRC §3111) on non-deductible compensation remains deductible as an
  ordinary business expense under IRC §162(a) — the §162(m) limitation applies only to
  "applicable employee remuneration," not to payroll taxes. The employer cost formula
  correctly separates these: grossCost = comp × (1 + ficaEmr); taxSavings = (deductible
  + comp × ficaEmr) × corporateRate; net = grossCost − taxSavings.

SECTION 2 — FICA / PAYROLL TAXES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

2.1 Social Security Tax (employee)
  Calculation: ficaBlended = 7.65% on wages up to ssWageBase; 1.45% on wages above
  Authority:
    - IRC §3101(a): 6.2% Social Security tax on employee wages (OASDI component)
    - IRC §3101(b): 1.45% Medicare tax on all employee wages (HI component)
    - IRC §3121(a)(1): Social Security wage base cap; §3121(a)(1) excludes wages above
      the annual contribution/benefit base from OASDI portion only
    - Combined employee FICA = 6.2% + 1.45% = 7.65% on wages up to the wage base;
      1.45% only on wages above
  Note: The tool uses 7.65% as the single input (ficaRate) and drops to 1.45% above
  the wage base. This correctly reflects the statutory structure.

2.2 Social Security Tax (employer)
  Calculation: same blending as employee side
  Authority:
    - IRC §3111(a): 6.2% employer OASDI on wages up to wage base
    - IRC §3111(b): 1.45% employer Medicare on all wages
    - Treas. Reg. §31.3111-1

2.3 Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%)
  Calculation: 0.9% × max(0, wages − $200,000); employee only
  Authority:
    - IRC §3101(b)(2): additional 0.9% HI tax on wages exceeding threshold (added by
      ACA, P.L. 111-148, effective 2013)
    - Threshold: $200,000 (single); $250,000 (MFJ); the tool defaults to $200k
    - Employee-only; §3111 has no corresponding employer obligation
    - IRS Publication 15 (Circular E), Section 16 for withholding rules
  Note: Correctly excluded from employer FICA (employerFicaEffective in tax.ts)

2.4 Social Security Wage Base Default
  Default value in types.ts: $168,600
  Authority: IRC §3121(a)(1); SSA announces the base annually per the cost-of-living
  adjustment formula in §230 of the Social Security Act
  FLAG #3: The default wage base of $168,600 is the 2024 value. The 2025 wage
  base was $176,100 (SSA announcement, October 2024). The 2026 value should be
  verified with the SSA announcement expected in October 2025. This default is
  stale by at least two years. Users who do not manually update this field will
  have their FICA calculations understated.

2.5 Cross-Component FICA Accumulation — FIXED
  The SS wage base cap under IRC §3121(a)(1) applies to the AGGREGATE of all FICA
  wages from a single employer in a calendar year. The engine now uses marginal FICA
  rates that respect cumulative year-to-date wages via the YearContext accumulator.
  Implementation: employeeFicaMarginalRate() and employerFicaMarginalRate() in tax.ts
  compute the marginal FICA on each new component given priorWages already accrued.
  YearContext.ficaWagesAccrued is reset to 0 each year in index.ts and threaded through
  every component in processing order. Example: $200k salary + $300k RSU vest in year 1.
  After salary: ficaWagesAccrued = $200k. RSU FICA uses priorWages = $200k, so only the
  increment between $200k and the SS wage base (and 1.45% above) applies to the RSU —
  correctly preventing double-counting of the below-wage-base portion.
  Authority: IRC §3121(a)(1); Treas. Reg. §31.3121(a)-1.

SECTION 3 — RESTRICTED STOCK (§83)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

3.1 Restricted Stock — No 83(b) Election — Income at Vest
  Calculation: fmvAtVest × sharesVesting × (1 − ordinaryRate − stateRate − ficaEmployee)
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(a): property transferred in connection with services is included in gross
      income when the property is substantially vested (no longer subject to a substantial
      risk of forfeiture or restriction on transfer)
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-1(a): income recognized in the first taxable year in which the
      property is substantially vested
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-3(b): "substantial risk of forfeiture" defined (employment
      conditions, performance conditions)
    - Income amount = FMV at vesting date less any amount paid for the property
    - Character: ordinary income (wages for W-2 purposes)
  Employer side: deduction at vest, same amount
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(h): employer deduction equals the amount includible in employee income;
      timing matches employee recognition year

3.2 Restricted Stock — With 83(b) Election — Income at Grant
  Calculation: fmvAtGrant × sharesGranted × (1 − ordinaryRate − stateRate − ficaEmployee)
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(b): election to include property in income in the year of transfer at
      its FMV (less any amount paid), even though it is subject to a substantial risk
      of forfeiture
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-2: requirements — must be filed with the IRS within 30 days
      of the date of transfer; copy attached to return
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-2(b): 30-day deadline is strict; no extensions granted (IRS
      PLR notwithstanding, late elections are generally invalid)
    - IRS Rev. Proc. 2012-29: current 83(b) election filing procedure
  FLAG #5 — OPERATIONAL LIMITATION: The tool does not warn the user that an 83(b)
  election must be filed within 30 days of the grant date. This is a critical,
  irrevocable decision with a strict statutory deadline under Treas. Reg. §1.83-2(b).
  A user relying on this tool who misses the 30-day window cannot make the election
  and will be taxed at vest instead. The tool should surface this warning prominently
  on RS components with election83b = true.

3.3 Restricted Stock — With 83(b) — LTCG at Vest Tranches
  Calculation: (fmvAtVest − fmvAtGrant) × sharesVesting × (1 − effectiveLTCGRate)
    when vestYear − grantYear >= 1 (long-term), otherwise ordinary rates
  Authority:
    - IRC §1222(3): long-term capital gain = gain on capital asset held >1 year
    - IRC §83(f): for property subject to a §83(b) election, the holding period
      begins on the date the property is transferred (i.e., grant date)
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-4(b): basis equals the amount included in income at grant
      plus any amount paid; subsequent gain is capital gain (long or short term)
    - IRC §1(h): federal LTCG rates (0%, 15%, 20%)
  Note: Year-level holding period comparison (vestYear − grantYear >= 1) is correct
  in principle but uses calendar year, not exact dates. Edge case: if a grant is
  on December 31, Year 1, and vest is on January 1, Year 2, the year difference
  is 1 but the actual holding period is 2 days — not long-term. The tool would
  incorrectly apply LTCG rates in this edge case. This is a known simplification.

3.4 Restricted Stock — Capital Loss Treatment
  Calculation: employeeAfterTaxCash = -|loss| + |loss| × (federalLTCGRate + stateLTCGRate)
  Authority:
    - IRC §1211(b): individuals may deduct capital losses only to the extent of
      capital gains, plus up to $3,000 against ordinary income
    - IRC §1212(b): net capital losses exceeding the annual limit carry forward
  FLAG #6 — MATERIAL SIMPLIFICATION: The tool assumes the full capital loss is
  immediately deductible (i.e., the taxpayer has sufficient capital gains to absorb
  the loss in the same year). Under §1211(b), if the employee has no other capital
  gains in that year, only $3,000 of the loss is deductible against ordinary income
  and the remainder carries forward. The tool overstates the immediate tax benefit
  of capital losses in years where the employee lacks offsetting capital gains.

SECTION 4 — RSUs (RESTRICTED STOCK UNITS)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

4.1 RSU Income Recognition at Vest (Delivery)
  Calculation: fmvAtVest × sharesVesting × (1 − ordinaryRate − stateRate − ficaEmployee)
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(a): RSUs are a contractual right (not a property transfer) until
      settlement/delivery; income recognized when shares are actually delivered
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-3(e): RSUs are generally not "property" under §83 until
      delivery — the employee holds an unfunded, unsecured promise to receive property
    - Rev. Rul. 2014-18: confirms that RSUs are not property for §83 purposes at grant;
      §83(b) elections are therefore not available for RSUs (no property is transferred
      at grant to trigger the election)
    - FICA timing: Rev. Rul. 2012-18 clarified that FICA wages for RSUs are recognized
      at delivery/settlement, NOT at grant; the general timing rule of §3121(v)(2) for
      NQDC does not apply to RSUs paid entirely in the year of vesting

4.2 No 83(b) Election for RSUs
  The tool correctly disables the 83(b) election flag for RSU components. Verified:
  Rev. Rul. 2014-18; IRS Publication 525 (Taxable and Nontaxable Income).

4.3 Double-Trigger RSU Deferral
  Calculation: income deferred to liquidityEventYear; FMV at liquidity event used
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(a): income recognized when property becomes substantially vested;
      a liquidity-event condition (e.g., IPO or acquisition required for settlement)
      constitutes a substantial risk of forfeiture under §83
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-3(c): conditions relating to the purpose of the transfer
      (here, employment + liquidity event) constitute SRF
  FLAG #7 — §409A RISK NOT MODELED: Double-trigger RSUs with a liquidity event
  condition must be structured to comply with IRC §409A (nonqualified deferred
  compensation rules), or the employee faces a 20% excise tax plus interest on the
  deferred amount under §409A(a)(1)(B). Specifically, the liquidity event must qualify
  as a "change in control" or other permissible §409A payment event, AND the RSU
  arrangement must specify a payment date at or shortly after the event. The tool
  models the tax math but does not evaluate or warn about §409A compliance risk.
  In practice, most pre-IPO double-trigger RSUs are drafted to comply with §409A,
  but users should verify this with counsel.

4.4 RSU Employer Deduction — FICA Timing
  Authority: IRC §83(h); Rev. Rul. 2012-18 (employer also recognizes the FICA
  obligation and deduction at delivery); Treas. Reg. §1.83-6(a) (employer's deduction
  tied to employee's inclusion)

SECTION 5 — INCENTIVE STOCK OPTIONS (ISOs)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

5.1 ISO Statutory Requirements
  Authority: IRC §422(b): options must meet all of the following to qualify as ISOs:
    (1) granted pursuant to a plan adopted by the corporation and approved by shareholders
    (2) granted within 10 years of plan adoption/approval
    (3) not exercisable more than 10 years from grant
    (4) strike price ≥ FMV at grant (110% of FMV for >10% shareholders, 5-year expiration)
    (5) option not transferable except at death
    (6) employee must be employed from grant through 3 months before exercise
  The tool does not validate requirements (1), (2), (5), or (6). It enforces (3)
  through the expirationDate field and warns if planned exercises exceed grant + 10 years.

5.2 ISO Disqualifying Disposition — Employee Tax
  Calculation: (fmvAtExercise − strikePrice) × shares × (1 − ordinaryRate − stateRate)
  Authority:
    - IRC §421(b): if ISO shares are sold or disposed of before satisfying the §422
      holding period (>1 year from exercise AND >2 years from grant), the employee
      recognizes ordinary income equal to the lesser of: (a) the FMV at exercise minus
      the strike price, or (b) the amount realized minus the strike price
    - For sell-on-exercise (sale price = FMV at exercise), these are equal: ordinary
      income = (FMVexercise − strike) × shares; no additional capital gain
    - Treas. Reg. §1.422-1(b): disqualifying disposition rule
    - The sell-on-exercise simplification (sale simultaneously with exercise) correctly
      makes capital gain = $0, because cost basis = FMV at exercise
  Note: No FICA is applied to ISO spread. See 5.3 below.

5.3 FICA on ISO Exercises — FIXED
  No FICA is applied to ISO exercises (employee or employer) per IRC §3121(a)(22):
    "(22) any remuneration on account of—
      (A) a transfer of a share of stock to any individual pursuant to an exercise
          of an incentive stock option (as defined in section 422(b)), or
      (B) any disposition by the individual of such share"
  shall not be treated as "wages" for FICA purposes.
  This statutory exemption applies to both the exercise event AND any subsequent
  disposition, including disqualifying dispositions. IRS Notice 2002-47 confirmed
  the exemption and announced a moratorium on FICA collection for ISO exercises
  pending further guidance; no regulations have been issued to override §3121(a)(22).
  The engine (iso.ts) correctly reflects: ficaWagesAdded = 0; no ficaEmp or ficaEmr
  terms in the employee or employer cost formulas.
  This asymmetry with NQOs (which ARE subject to FICA per Rev. Rul. 2012-18 and
  IRC §3101/§3111) is intentional under the statute and is a key tax planning
  distinction between the two option types.

5.4 ISO $100k Annual Limit
  Calculation: if sharesGranted × grantFMV > $100,000, excess shares treated as NQOs
  Authority:
    - IRC §422(d): to the extent the aggregate FMV of ISO shares "first becoming
      exercisable" in any calendar year exceeds $100,000 (measured at grant-date FMV),
      the excess is treated as NQOs
  FLAG #9 — KNOWN SIMPLIFICATION: The tool applies the $100k test at the total-grant
  level (sharesGranted × grantFMV) rather than the per-year-first-becoming-exercisable
  level required by §422(d). This is acknowledged in SPEC.md as a v1 simplification.
  The per-year exercisable test could produce materially different ISO/NQO splits for
  options with multi-year vesting (e.g., 4-year ratable vesting means only 25% of
  shares first become exercisable per year, so the $100k limit applies to 25% of
  shares per year, not the full grant). The current implementation applies a more
  conservative (larger NQO bucket) test at the grant level.
  The SPEC correctly describes this as a simplification. For small grants relative to
  $100k, the error is zero. For large grants (common in startup executive packages),
  the error could significantly mischaracterize the ISO vs. NQO split.

5.5 ISO Qualified Dispositions — Out of Scope
  The tool does not model qualifying dispositions (hold >1 year from exercise AND
  >2 years from grant) in v1. For a qualifying ISO disposition:
    - Employee pays LTCG on the entire spread (IRC §422(a)(1))
    - Employer receives NO compensation deduction (IRC §421(a)(2))
    - No AMT preference item if disqualifying (but qualifying dispositions create
      an AMT preference item for the spread under IRC §56(b)(3))
  This is the most tax-advantaged ISO scenario and is entirely absent from v1.
  The tool's inability to model qualifying dispositions means it cannot properly
  compare the ISO-as-LTCG strategy to NQO or RSU alternatives — which is arguably
  the most important planning question for option holders. This is a fundamental
  limitation for the compensation planning use case.
  Authority: IRC §422(a); §421(a)(2); §56(b)(3); Treas. Reg. §1.422-1

5.6 AMT on ISO Exercises
  The tool models AMT = $0 for all ISO exercises (correct for disqualifying dispositions
  under the sell-on-exercise assumption).
  Authority: IRC §56(b)(3): the spread on an ISO exercise (FMV at exercise minus
  strike price) is an AMT preference item — but ONLY for qualifying disposition ISOs
  (hold-and-exercise). For a disqualifying disposition in the same year as exercise,
  the spread is ordinary income and is NOT an AMT preference item.
  This logic is correct for v1's sell-on-exercise assumption.

5.7 Employer Deduction for ISO Disqualifying Dispositions — FIXED
  Calculation: spread − deductible × corporateRate  (no employer FICA)
  Authority:
    - IRC §421(b): upon a disqualifying disposition, the employer is entitled to
      a deduction under §162 equal to the ordinary income recognized by the employee
    - IRC §83(h) does not apply to ISOs (§83(e)(1) excludes transfers covered by §421);
      the disqualifying disposition exception in §421(b) separately allows the deduction
    - The deduction amount is the lesser of: (a) income recognized by employee, or
      (b) gain realized on the sale. For sell-on-exercise, these are equal.
    - No employer FICA: §3121(a)(22) exemption applies to employer side as well;
      therefore no ficaEmr term and no FICA deductibility adjustment needed
    - §162(m) cap applied via deductibleCompFor() for public company scenarios

SECTION 6 — NON-QUALIFIED OPTIONS (NQOs)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

6.1 NQO Income at Exercise — Employee
  Calculation: (fmvAtExercise − strikePrice) × shares × (1 − ordinaryRate − stateRate − fica)
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(a): the spread on NQO exercise is ordinary income in the year of exercise
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-7(a): specifically addresses options without a readily
      ascertainable FMV at grant (the typical private-company scenario); income
      recognized on exercise
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-7(b): options with a readily ascertainable FMV at grant would
      be taxed at grant — but this is rare for employee options and not modeled

6.2 FICA on NQO Exercise
  Calculation: FICA applied to spread at exercise (employee and employer)
  Authority:
    - Rev. Rul. 2012-18: clarified that NQO exercise spread is subject to FICA
      at the time of exercise; the spread is "wages" under §3121(a) because it
      arises from employment
    - IRC §3101; §3111; Rev. Rul. 2004-60 (earlier guidance on FICA/FUTA timing)
  Note: Correct. NQO FICA treatment is the opposite of ISO (§3121(a)(22)) and
  this distinction is important for the employer's negotiated choice between NQOs
  and ISOs.

6.3 Employer Deduction for NQO Exercise
  Calculation: spread × (1 + ficaEmployer) × (1 − corporateRate)
  Authority:
    - IRC §83(h): employer deduction equals the amount includible in employee's income
    - Deduction timing: same year employee recognizes income (§83(h))
    - Treas. Reg. §1.83-6(a)(1): deduction taken in taxable year that includes the
      day on which the amount is includible in employee's income

6.4 §409A and NQOs
  NQOs granted at or above FMV (with no deferral feature) are generally exempt from
  §409A under Treas. Reg. §1.409A-1(b)(5). The tool does not warn if the user inputs
  a strike price below FMV, which could create §409A exposure. FLAG #10: The tool
  warns that ISO strike > FMV is unusual but does not warn about the §409A implications
  of a below-FMV NQO strike price. For NQOs, a below-FMV strike creates §409A
  liability for the employee: 20% excise tax on deferred amounts plus an underpayment
  interest penalty (IRC §409A(a)(1)(B)).

SECTION 7 — LONG-TERM CAPITAL GAINS & NIIT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

7.1 LTCG Rates
  effectiveLTCGRate = federalLTCGRate + stateLTCGRate + (niitAlwaysOn ? niitRate : 0)
  Authority:
    - IRC §1(h): preferential rates on net capital gain (0%, 15%, 20%) based on
      taxable income thresholds; the 20% rate applies to taxpayers in the top ordinary
      income bracket
    - IRC §1(h)(11): qualified dividend income taxed at same LTCG rates
  Note: The tool uses a single flat federalLTCGRate input, which the user sets to
  the applicable rate (0%, 15%, or 20%). This is correct for a marginal analysis.

7.2 Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)
  Authority: IRC §1411(a): 3.8% tax on lesser of (a) net investment income or
  (b) excess of MAGI over threshold ($200k single / $250k MFJ)
  Net investment income under §1411(c) includes: capital gains, interest, dividends,
  rental income, passive activity income. It does NOT include wages, active business
  income, NQO exercise spread, RSU vest income, or RS vest income (ordinary income
  from services).
  The tool correctly limits NIIT to LTCG events (via effectiveLTCGRate) and does not
  apply NIIT to ordinary income components such as salary, bonus, RSU vest, RS vest,
  NQO spread, or ISO disqualifying disposition spread.
  Known simplification: The niitAlwaysOn toggle bypasses the MAGI threshold gating —
  a user earning below the NIIT threshold would overstate their tax by toggling this on.
  The tool documents this limitation in the SPEC.

7.3 NIIT on Capital Losses
  The tool correctly excludes niitRate from the capital loss tax benefit calculation
  (capitalLossRate in tax.ts returns only federalLTCGRate + stateLTCGRate).
  Authority: NIIT under §1411 applies to net investment income; a net capital loss
  reduces net investment income but does not independently generate a negative NIIT
  refund — the floor is $0. Using niitRate = 0 for capital loss benefit calculation
  is correct.

SECTION 8 — CORPORATE TAX / EMPLOYER DEDUCTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

8.1 Corporate Tax Rate
  Default: 21%
  Authority: IRC §11(b): flat 21% rate on C-corporation taxable income (TCJA §13001,
  effective 2018; made permanent by TCJA with no sunset)
  Note: As of the current date (May 2026), no legislation has changed the 21% rate,
  though the "One Big Beautiful Bill" proposed in 2025 included potential corporate rate
  modifications. Users should verify current corporate rate.

8.2 Employer Net Cost Formula — UPDATED
  Formula: grossCost − taxSavings
    grossCost  = comp × (1 + ficaEmr)    [ficaEmr = 0 for ISO per §3121(a)(22)]
    taxSavings = (deductible + comp × ficaEmr) × corporateRate
    where deductible = deductibleCompFor(comp, priorDeductibleComp, taxInputs)
  This formulation correctly separates the §162(m)-limited compensation deduction
  from the always-deductible employer FICA, avoiding the prior error of applying
  the corporate tax shield only to the "deductible × (1 + ficaEmr)" portion.
    - Employer FICA on non-deductible comp IS still deductible under IRC §162(a)
      (it is a payroll tax, not "applicable employee remuneration" subject to §162(m))
    - §162(m) limits the comp deduction only; it does not limit the FICA deduction
  Authority:
    - IRC §162(a): ordinary and necessary business expense; employer FICA deductible
    - IRC §162(m): limits only "applicable employee remuneration"
    - Treas. Reg. §1.162-1: FICA as deductible business expense

8.3 RS with 83(b) — Employer Deduction Timing
  Calculation: employer deducts at grant (not at vest)
  Authority: IRC §83(h): employer's deduction is in the taxable year in which or
  with which ends the taxable year of the employee in which the amount is included
  in income. For 83(b) elections, the employee includes income at grant; the employer
  deducts in the same year.
  Note: This is correctly implemented — rs83bGrant generates an employerNetCost in
  the grant year only; rs83bVest generates employerNetCost = 0 (no further employer
  deduction at vest for 83(b) RS).

SECTION 9 — NPV / DISCOUNTING
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

9.1 NPV Calculation
  Formula: NPV = Σ [ CF_t / (1 + r)^t ] for t = 1 to horizon
  Authority: Standard finance theory (Modigliani-Miller; Graham & Harvey on hurdle
  rates). No IRC authority required for the discounting mechanics.
  Note: The NPV formula in npv.ts uses (i+1) as the exponent for index i (0-based),
  meaning year 1 cash flows are discounted by (1+r)^1. This is correct for end-of-year
  cash flow timing (standard annuity convention).

9.2 Employee Discount Rate
  Represents the employee's personal required rate of return (opportunity cost of
  capital). This is a user input with no prescribed value. Users should set this
  to reflect their personal risk preference and alternative investment options.

9.3 Employer Discount Rate
  Represents the company's WACC or hurdle rate. User input. For private startups,
  this is typically higher than public company rates (15–25% is common). The tool
  does not suggest a default, which is appropriate.

SECTION 10 — RATES & DEFAULT VALUES: CURRENCY AND ACCURACY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The following default values in types.ts (DEFAULT_TAX_INPUTS) were verified against
primary sources and flagged where stale or potentially incorrect:

  federalOrdinaryRate: 0.37
    Verified: IRC §1(j)(2)(D) as amended by TCJA; top rate is 37% for 2018–2025.
    FLAG #1 — 2026 TCJA SUNSET: The TCJA individual tax provisions (including the

FLAG #1 — TCJA SUNSET [RESOLVED]
  The TCJA individual rate provisions were extended by Congress and remain in effect
  for tax year 2026. The default federalOrdinaryRate of 0.37 is correct.
  Authority: IRC §1(j)(2)(D); enacted extension confirmed May 2026.

FLAG #2 — §162(m) NOT APPLIED TO EQUITY COMPENSATION [RESOLVED]
  Fixed. The §162(m) $1M cap is now applied cumulatively across ALL compensation types
  (salary, bonus, RSU vest, NQO exercise, RS vest, ISO disqualifying disposition) via
  the YearContext.deductibleCompAccrued accumulator and deductibleCompFor() helper.
  See Section 1.3 for updated calculation details.
  Authority: IRC §162(m) as amended by TCJA §13601; IRS Notice 2018-68.

FLAG #3 — STALE SS WAGE BASE DEFAULT [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  Default ssWageBase of $168,600 is the 2024 value. Current value for 2025 is
  $176,100. 2026 value should be verified with SSA announcement. Users who do
  not manually update this will have FICA calculations slightly overstated (too
  much wages taxed at 7.65% rather than 1.45%).
  Authority: IRC §3121(a)(1); SSA annual announcement.

FLAG #4 — FICA WAGE BASE NOT ACCUMULATED ACROSS COMPONENTS [RESOLVED]
  Fixed. employeeFicaMarginalRate() and employerFicaMarginalRate() in tax.ts now
  compute marginal FICA given year-to-date wages already accrued. YearContext
  accumulates ficaWagesAccrued across components per year in index.ts. See
  Section 2.5 for details.
  Authority: IRC §3121(a)(1); Treas. Reg. §31.3121(a)-1.

FLAG #5 — NO 83(b) DEADLINE WARNING [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  The tool does not alert users that a §83(b) election must be filed within 30 days
  of the grant date. Late elections are invalid. This is an operational risk, not a
  calculation error, but could cause significant tax harm if a user configures an
  83(b) election assuming they made it, when in fact they have not.
  Authority: Treas. Reg. §1.83-2(b).

FLAG #6 — CAPITAL LOSS FULL DEDUCTIBILITY ASSUMED [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  The tax benefit on RS 83(b) capital losses is computed as the full loss times the
  LTCG rate, implicitly assuming the taxpayer has sufficient capital gains to absorb
  the loss. Under §1211(b), only $3,000 of net capital losses can offset ordinary
  income annually; the remainder carries forward. Outputs for scenarios with capital
  losses should be treated as upper bounds.
  Authority: IRC §1211(b); §1212(b).

FLAG #7 — §409A RISK ON DOUBLE-TRIGGER RSUs NOT MODELED [MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  Double-trigger RSU arrangements must comply with §409A. Non-compliance triggers a
  20% excise tax plus interest on the deferred amount. The tool models the income tax
  math but does not evaluate §409A compliance. Users should ensure their RSU plan
  documents are reviewed by counsel.
  Authority: IRC §409A; Treas. Reg. §1.409A-1 through §1.409A-6.

FLAG #8 — FICA APPLIED TO ISO DISQUALIFYING DISPOSITIONS [RESOLVED]
  Fixed. FICA has been removed from ISO calculations (employee and employer) in iso.ts.
  ficaWagesAdded = 0 for all ISO exercises. Employer cost formula uses no ficaEmr term.
  The §3121(a)(22) exemption covers both the exercise and any disposition.
  Authority: IRC §3121(a)(22); IRS Notice 2002-47.

FLAG #9 — ISO $100k LIMIT USES GRANT-LEVEL TEST [LOW-MEDIUM PRIORITY]
  Acknowledged simplification: the per-year-first-becoming-exercisable test required
  by §422(d) is more favorable to the taxpayer than the grant-level test used. For
  multi-year vesting schedules, the tool may over-convert ISO shares to NQO treatment.
  Authority: IRC §422(d). Planned for v1.1 per SPEC.

FLAG #10 — BELOW-FMV NQO STRIKE PRICE §409A RISK NOT WARNED [LOW PRIORITY]
  An NQO with a strike price below FMV at grant is subject to §409A penalties.
  The tool does not warn about this if the user enters strikePrice < grantFMV for NQOs.
  Authority: Treas. Reg. §1.409A-1(b)(5)(i)(A).

FLAG #11 — CALIFORNIA HIGH-INCOME SURTAX NOT IN DEFAULT [LOW PRIORITY]
  California's 1% Mental Health Services Tax (Prop. 63) applies to income over $1M,
  making the effective top CA rate 13.3%, not 9.3%. The default stateOrdinaryRate of
  0.093 understates CA taxes for high-income executives.
  Authority: CA Rev. & Tax. Code §17043.
